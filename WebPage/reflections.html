Reflections on Fall 2018, an HTML Perspective

It was an interesting start to 2018. Starting up at school again always presents challenges. The books! The deadlines! The coffee! While the pace can be relentless, it is always a good idea to pause and think about what has been accomplished, what has differed from expectations, and what is still to come.

I first programmed a webpage in 1998. I wrote my last legacy HTML in 2000. Since then, I've been building things that didn't require a computer (see my resume for details). Things have moved on again, and the new Era of Nate™ dictates going back inside and maximizing possibilities. In short, I am back on the computer, kicking the rust off the keyboard and dusting off my mouse.

To refine skills long dormant I've chosen Edmonds Community College's Web Dev and Cloud App degree for my coursework, and after a year, there is real progress! The first step has been unlearning a lot of the code that is no longer required or done in different ways now.

Page structure when I was teaching myself HTML in the 90's was a single column of items. Sidebars and navigation panels were not common, and typically took messy tables or frames before CSS first became available. While they worked and certainly paved the way for Web 2.0 and the dynamic sites of the modern era, it was fiddly, and difficult to reproduce across platforms and display sizes. For example, this article is using the <article> tag to put it below my <header> section at the top of the page. The <footer> follows down at the bottom. If I wanted to, this would be a very small amount of coding for the basic structure. When combined with the twice-revised CSS 3, style and placement of elements, even on diverse layouts and display sizes, is not nearly as complicated, and the results you see before you. My style, my way, and you can do it that way, too! Try taking a course at EdCC, or check out the CIW Internet Business Associate certificate.

I find my attention falls most heavily on scripting languages and those things "pure" HTML doesn't do. In my first iteration as a web-dev, scripting and dynamically-generated content was just beginning to hit its stride. To see it now, fleshed out, is quite interesting. The old players like Perl and PHP are there of course, but new-to-me things like JavaScript and Database linkages are fascinating and powerful tools.
